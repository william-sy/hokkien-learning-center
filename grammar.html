📖 Hokkien Grammar Guide

Hokkien (閩南語 Bân-lâm-gú) is an analytic language — verbs never conjugate for tense or person, and nouns don't change for plural. Meaning is expressed through word order, time words, and particles. This guide uses POJ (Pe̍h-ōe-jī) romanization throughout. For pronunciation and tone charts see the 🎵 Tone Chart page.

⏱ Tense & Time

Hokkien has no grammatical tense — verbs do not change form to indicate past, present, or future. Instead, time is conveyed with time adverbs placed before or at the start of the sentence.

💡 The same verb form is used regardless of time. Context or a time word does the work.

🔵 Aspect Particles

While Hokkien lacks tense, it has a rich system of aspect particles that express whether an action is ongoing, completed, or has been experienced. These are the closest equivalent to English "-ing" or "have done".

ParticlePOJMeaningExample (POJ)English
有ū Completed — "did / have done" Guá ū khì. I went / I have been there.
咧 / tehteh Progressive — "is/am/are doing" Guá teh tsia̍h. I am eating.
矣 (–ah)--ah Completive / new situation — sentence-final I khì--ah. He has gone (now).
過 (–kòe)--kòe Experiential — "have ever done" Guá tsia̍h--kòe hit-ê. I've eaten that before.
著 (–tio̍h)--tio̍h Resultative — "successfully did / accidentally did" Guá tshuē--tio̍h i. I found him (managed to).
毋捌 / batbat Experiential past — "once / used to" Guá bat khì Tâi-oân. I've been to Taiwan (before).
💡 ū vs. --ah: 有 (ū) is preverbal and focuses on whether the action occurred. 矣 (--ah) is sentence-final and signals a change of state or a new situation.
• 我有食 (Guá ū tsia̍h) — "I ate / I did eat" (confirming the action)
• 我食矣 (Guá tsia̍h--ah) — "I've already eaten" (I wasn't eating before, now I have)

Negative aspect

我無去。 Guá bô khì. I didn't go. (negation of 有)
我毋捌去過。 Guá m̄-bat khì--kòe. I have never been there.

🔢 Plural & Classifiers

Hokkien nouns have no plural form. Quantity is expressed with numbers + classifiers (measure words), or with words meaning "all" or "every". A classifier must appear between a number and the noun.

👤 Pronouns

One important feature of Hokkien is the inclusive/exclusive "we" distinction: 咱 (lán) includes the listener; 阮 (gún) excludes them.

Possessives

Add 的 (ê) after a pronoun to show possession (equivalent to 's or "of"):

我的冊 guá ê chheh my book
伊的厝 i ê chhù his/her house
咱的語言 lán ê gí-giân our language (inclusive)

🚫 Negation

Hokkien has several negation words, each used in different contexts. Choosing the wrong one is a very common learner mistake.

WordPOJUseExample (POJ)English
無bô Negates existence, possession, or a completed action Guá bô tsîⁿ. I have no money.
毋m̄ Negates present/habitual actions and states Guá m̄ khì. I'm not going / I won't go.
毋是m̄-sī "Is not" — negates identity I m̄-sī ha̍k-seng. He is not a student.
袂 / 袂曉bē / bē-hiáu "Cannot" — inability Guá bē-hiáu kóng. I can't speak (it).
莫 / 毋通mài / m̄-thang Imperative — "Don't!" (prohibition) Mài khì! Don't go!
毋捌m̄-bat "Have never / don't know" (experiential negative) Guá m̄-bat tsia̍h--kòe. I've never eaten it.
💡 無 vs. 毋: 無 (bô) is for what doesn't exist or didn't happen (past/state). 毋 (m̄) is for what you don't do or aren't doing (present/habitual).

• 我無去 (Guá bô khì) = I didn't go (past)
• 我毋去 (Guá m̄ khì) = I'm not going / I refuse to go (present/future)

❓ Questions

Hokkien forms yes/no questions using the A-not-A pattern, and content questions using question words. Unlike Mandarin, there is no sentence-final question particle 嗎 — the A-not-A pattern or rising intonation is used instead.

Comparative (simple) A + 較 (khah) + adj I khah tuā. 伊較大。 He/she is bigger/older.
Comparative (explicit "than") A + 比 (pí) + B + 較 + adj I pí guá khah tuā. 伊比我較大。 He/she is bigger than me.
Superlative 上 (siōng) / 最 (tsòe) + adj I siōng suí. 伊上媠。 She is the most beautiful.

💡 較 (khah) can also simply mean "rather / quite" without an explicit comparison:
• 今仔日較冷。(Kin-á-ji̍t khah líng.) — It's rather cold today.

🔁 Reduplication

Reduplication (repeating a syllable or word) is very productive in Hokkien. It softens verbs, intensifies adjectives, and forms distributive expressions.

Adjective AA Intensifies 好 (hó) — good 好好 (hó-hó) very well / properly
Adjective AA Intensifies 細 (sè) — small 細細 (sè-sè) very small / little by little
Verb + 咧 (A-A-leh) Softens — "just do a bit" 看 (khòaⁿ) — look 看看咧 (khòaⁿ-khòaⁿ-leh) just take a look

💬 Sentence-Final Particles

Sentence-final particles are a defining feature of Hokkien. They carry emotional and pragmatic meaning — signalling completion, softening a command, expressing surprise, or marking shared knowledge. They attach to the end of a sentence with a light tone.

ParticlePOJMeaning / FeelExampleEnglish
矣--ah New situation / completion — "now, already" I khì--ah. He's gone (now).
啦--lah Softening / mild assertion — "come on, it's fine" Hó--lah. It's fine / OK lah.
咧--leh Ongoing / mild insistence — "it's happening, you know" I teh tsia̍h--leh. He's eating (can't you see?).
哦--oh Mild surprise or new information — "oh, is that so" I sī lāu-su--oh. Oh, he's a teacher!
哩--lih Tag question / confirmation-seeking (Taiwanese) Lí ū--lih? You do have one, right?
罷--pah Concessive / resigned — "I suppose, whatever" Án-ne--pah. I suppose that's how it is.
喔--o͘h Warning / reminder — "you know / be careful" Mài khì--o͘h. Don't go, OK? (warning)
💡 Particles can stack in informal speech: 好啦矣 (Hó--lah--ah) = "Alright already!" The order and combination varies by dialect region.

🔤 Word Order

Hokkien follows a basic Subject – Verb – Object (SVO) order, similar to English and Mandarin. However, Hokkien also makes heavy use of topic-comment structures, where the topic is fronted.

Basic SVO

我愛伊。 Guá ài i. I love him/her. (S + V + O)
伊食一碗飯。 I tsia̍h tsi̍t uánn pn̄g. He/she ate a bowl of rice.

Topic-Comment (topic fronted)

The topic (what you're talking about) comes first, then a comment about it:

這本冊，我看過矣。 Chit-pún chheh, guá khòaⁿ--kòe--ah. This book, I've already read it.
伊的話，我毋信。 I ê uē, guá m̄-sìn. What he says, I don't believe.

Time and Place Adverbs

Time words typically come at the start of the sentence or directly before the verb. Place words with 佇 (tī, "at/in") usually come before the verb as well.

伊佇厝裡食飯。 I tī chhù-lāi tsia̍h pn̄g. He eats at home. (place before verb)
我明仔再欲去台灣。 Guá bîn-á-chài beh khì Tâi-oân. I'm going to Taiwan tomorrow.
